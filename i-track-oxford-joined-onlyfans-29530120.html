But the Geordie lass, who was working 14 hour shifts seven days a week by combining four jobs, chose a saucier route.

“I couldn’t think of doing anything else now and I never want to work a normal job again that’s for sure. There are no regrets on my side.

“There is nothing more valuable than your time. And I have every day to myself. Don’t get me wrong, I have to work.

“I get up at 5am and I work until midnight pretty much every day replying to messages.

“People don’t see that side of it but I can get up and I can spend two hours at the gym if I want. I can spend two hours at the beach. I can do some shopping or I can see my friends, I can do that every day and I don’t have to book anything off work.

“That to me is more valuable than money, but obviously the money helps.”

As for the financial side, she added: “I’ve not been an overnight success.

“There were months where I made £800 and it has not always been in the thousands and thousands a month but that is the direction we are heading now and that’s where I want to keep it.”

April, 19, excelled in school and some of her pals predicted her to become a brain surgeon.

And an academic pathway was once the most likely option.

Remembering, she said: “I was dead clever in school and most people don’t expect that. I came out of my GSCEs with A stars in everything.

“I was proper clever and I was on schemes with Oxford and Cambridge. I absolutely wanted to go to uni and I was seriously in talks about going to the top universities in the country.

“But I went to college, and I think you can tell I am quite common, I just say it how it is.

“Everyone was dead posh and they would be like ‘oh this weekend I’ve got horse riding lessons and clarinet’ and I’d be like ‘I’m getting a f***ing takeaway tonight and I’m f***ing buzzing’ and they were looking at me like I was from a different planet.

“So after six weeks I thought I couldn’t gel with them so I quit.”

April, who is from Newcastle, then did a marketing apprenticeship before starting her own children’s entertainment business.

Laughing, she said: “It is strange that I went from being a children’s entertainer to an adult one.”

On weekdays, aged 17, she travelled two hours on a bus to work as a food tester from 9-1 and then travelled two hours to her next job at a call centre where she stayed until late.

Then at weekends she did shifts at a hair and makeup photography studio and then worked at kids parties in between her jobs.

Asked why she grafted so much, she said: “When you are on £4.60 an hour it takes a few jobs to keep a roof over your head when you have all these bills. I was left with £50 once bills were paid.”

At the time, she was living with her ex-boyfriend but moved home after the breakup.

To cope with her relentless responsibilities, April started a TikTok account as an escape.

She said: “I thought it was a crock of s*** at first but everyone was downloading. I got to 30,000 followers in my first month and I thought I needed to find a way to make money off this.

“That’s where OnlyFans came in. I just thought it made sense. You have to think about digital footprint but there are so many lasses who do it now.

“I thought even if I do it and it does t**s up and someone finds out along the way, there are many companies run by young people who know what this kind of stuff is and are not that bothered.

“But it has gone really well. I have had a year to get to grips with it and this year is all about scaling and I want to do this long term.”

And as for how her mother reacted to her OnlyFans career, she laughed: “She has always been ok with it, she is a bit like Kris Jenner when she found out Kim had a sex tape, that is my mam to a tee.”

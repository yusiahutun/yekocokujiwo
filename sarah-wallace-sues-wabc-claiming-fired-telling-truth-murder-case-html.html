Sarah Wallace sues WABC claiming she was 'fired for telling the truth' about murder case

April 02, 2024

Award-winning investigative reporter Sarah Wallace who was ousted by WABC last winter after 30 years is suing her former bosses accusing them of trying to stop her from 'telling the truthâ€™ about a murder case and claiming pay discrimination.

In her soon-to-be filed lawsuit, Wallace, 59, will allege that male colleagues of equal or lesser tenure, responsibilities and accolades had higher salaries compared to the Emmy- and Peabody-winning TV reporter.

Wallace's attorney, Joe Tacopina, filed preliminary paperwork with state Supreme Court in Manhattan Monday, claiming violations of the Federal Equal Pay, Labor Law and human rights laws.

Ousted: Sarah Wallace, 59, a winner of two Peabody awards and 13 Emmys (pictured here at the 50th Annual New York Emmy Awards Gala on April 1, 2007), left WABC in February after 30 years

Tacopina, a high-powered attorney who also represented New York Yankees third-baseman Alex Rodriguez, said his client was hoping to avoid a lawsuit, 'but some of the statements made by Channel 7 managers were so derogatory and offensive, she could not allow them to go unchecked.'

According to Page Six, Wallace's suit will also claim that her abrupt dismissal was prompted by her reporting on the case of Archie Cosey.

Cosey was sentenced to 25 years to life on murder and conspiracy charges after pleading guilty in 1998.

He later tried to rescind his plea, saying he had agreed to it under duress from his co-defendants, but a judge rejected his request.

According to Wallace' s lawyer, the veteran reporter uncovered new information about the case that could overturn Cosey's conviction, but her superiors wanted none of it.

'She was fired for telling the truth,' Tacopina said.

No love lost: Prior to her departure, Wallace (left) had been allegedly locking horns with her boss, the much-feared WABC News Director Camille Edwards (right)

The legal notice filed by the lawyer states that Wallace will be seeking a minimum of $200,000 in damages, as well as punitive damages, attorney's fees and related legal costs.

Over the course of her journalistic career, Wallace has racked up 13 Emmys, a Peabody Award and two Edward R. Murrow Awards for outstanding reporting.

During her tenure with Channel 7, Wallace had covered some of the biggest news stories in New York, including the September 11 terrorist attacks and, more recently, the police-involved choking death of unarmed black man Eric Garner.

The New York Daily News reported that prior to her departure, Wallace had been locking horns with her boss, the much-feared WABC News Director Camille Edwards - a claim the network denied.

Edwards has gained a reputation for being a tough manager at the station, where she has been dubbed 'Camevil.'

In April, the mother of WABC reporter Lisa Colagrossi, who died March 19 from a brain aneurysm while covering a story, publicly accused Edwards of working her daughter to death.

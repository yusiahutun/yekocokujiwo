Barry Hearn 'could smell money' before changing darts

July 15, 2024
blog

Barry Hearn has recounted the moment which inspired him to transform darts into the sporting juggernaut it is today.

It’s more than 20 years since the sports promoter took control of the PDC, with the governing body and the game in general flourishing and becoming more lucrative over the last two decades. Players from Phil Taylor to Michael van Gerwen have become household names, while fans watch in their millions at home and in their thousands at arenas nationwide.

The PDC moved its World Championship away from the legendary but cramped Circus Tavern to the Alexandra Palace, a move which has paid off handsomely since the switch in 2008. And it was a visit to the Circus Tavern in Purfleet which convinced Hearn he had to get involved in the sport.

Speaking on the Gary Newbon Sports Show with Jewson, Hearn said: “I walked into the Circus Tavern to watch the old world championships. The people that ran the game were trying to get me involved to handle some of their commercial work. And as I walked in, I just turned round and said ‘I just smell money, I just smell money.’

“Everyone looked at me and said ‘what do you mean?’ I see 800 of me in there. They’re having a cigarette, they’re having a beer, they’re having a bet, they’re eating a pizza, they’re having a laugh and a joke and they’re chanting.

“But, the kicker, they’re watching world-class sport. Let’s find a way where we capture all of that party atmosphere but get the game some respect so that people take it seriously; sponsors and broadcasters.”

Hearn, as the head of Matchroom Sport, was key to the boom of snooker in the 1980s and has also successfully promoted boxing for decades. But he rates his impact on darts as the great achievement of his career.

“I think it’s been my best lifetime achievement with what has happened to darts,” said Hearn, who stepped down as Matchroom chairman last year, becoming president in an advisory role. “It’s made up of ordinary people with extraordinary ability earning money they could only ever dream of. It’s not Premier League football, because that’s a world apart from anything else.

“But when you’re getting ordinary guys coming from the background that I came with, council houses or whatever, they haven’t got a lot of money, they might have been a builder, they might have worked in a pub, they might have been a car mechanic.

“But they had this God-given ability to play darts. What they needed was activity, regular work, prize money increases, and enough confidence in their own ability to go full time as a professional so they would be able to practice for longer hours and establish a greater degree of technical ability.”
